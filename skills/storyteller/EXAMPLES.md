Real essays written using this skill, with notes on what each one did well and what needed multiple passes.

## Example 1: Everyone Remembers the Thumb. Nobody Asks About the Clay Statue.

**Idea going in:** Note-taking while using AI keeps you actively engaged rather than passively reading outputs.

**Anchor story found:** Ekalavya from the Mahabharata — specifically the clay statue, not the thumb everyone remembers.

**The avoided sentence (Step 0):** "I was telling myself I was being efficient. What I was actually doing was outsourcing my thinking and calling it a system."

**Personal arc:**
- Origin: School notebooks I despised — copying vs reconstructing
- Carried: IIT prep (failed twice), working years
- Tested: COVID Zoom calls, phasing out on camera
- Named: Instagram reel about active recall
- New application: AI sycophancy and context anchoring

**What needed multiple passes:**
- The IIT section kept retreating from the failure — needed "I have made peace with the second failure. The first one I am still not sure about."
- Stakes were too vague — "hours of work" became "I called it letting AI do its thing. Hours later I had pages built entirely on what AI had silently decided I meant."
- No physical scene — added the summer morning classroom, AC as reward, chalk smell, hand moving while still half-dreaming.

**Final length:** ~1600 words + 2 postscripts

## Prompts that worked during this essay

After a long AI session, to surface hidden assumptions:
> "What assumptions have you made in this conversation that I did not explicitly state? List them."

To get a visual of AI's reasoning:
> "Summarise what you just did as a mind map."

To find context drift:
> "What is the earliest assumption in this conversation that everything else depends on?"
