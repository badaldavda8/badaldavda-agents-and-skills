# Storyteller — Personal Essay Writing Skill

Turn a raw idea, lived experience, or lesson into a structured personal essay — the way Badal writes them.

## What this skill does

You bring: a rough idea, a personal experience, a lesson you learned, or even just a feeling you want to write about.

This skill walks you through the full process — from finding the real emotional center, to finding the right anchor story, to structuring it like a storyteller, editing it like an editor, trimming to ~1500 words, and packaging what doesn't fit into a postscript. Research agents are used where needed. Nothing is made up.

---

## How the skill starts — the opening interview

When invoked, do not jump into writing. Ask these questions first, one at a time. Wait for the user's answer before asking the next. Do not rush. The answers to these questions *are* the essay — the writing comes later.

---

**Question 1 — The real reason (metacognition)**

> "Before we start writing, I want to understand why you want to write this at all. Not the topic — the reason. Who do you want to read this? What do you want them to feel or do differently after they have read it? And honestly — why does *you* writing this matter? Why are you the right person to tell this story?"

Listen carefully. If the answer is "I just think it's interesting" or "it might be useful to someone" — push gently:

> "What happened to *you* that made this worth writing about? Was there a moment — a failure, a surprise, a conversation — where this stopped being an idea and became something personal?"

Do not move on until the user has named a specific moment, not just a topic.

---

**Question 2 — The personal story behind the idea**

> "Tell me the story behind this — not the polished version, just what actually happened. Walk me through it chronologically. Where were you? What were you doing? What went wrong, or surprised you, or changed how you thought? Who else was there?"

Let the user talk. Take notes. Reflect back what you heard:

> "So if I understood correctly, the core of this is [restate in one sentence]. Is that right, or is there something more underneath it?"

Ask follow-ups that go deeper, not broader:
- *"What did that feel like in the moment?"*
- *"What did you almost do instead?"*
- *"What would have happened if you hadn't noticed this?"*
- *"Is there a part of this you haven't told anyone yet?"*

That last question often surfaces Step 0.

---

**Question 3 — The avoided sentence (emotional center)**

> "Here is a question most people skip. What is the one sentence in this essay you are most reluctant to write? The one that feels too exposed, too honest, too obvious, or too small to say out loud?"

If the user says they don't have one, try:

> "What is the thing about this story that you hope readers *don't* notice — or don't ask about? What would make you uncomfortable if someone read it and said 'yes, that part — tell me more about that'?"

That discomfort is almost always the center of the essay. Everything else is scaffolding around it.

---

**Question 4 — How the user wants it to feel (structure instinct)**

> "Before I suggest a structure — how do you imagine this reading? Is it a confession? A lesson? A journey from wrong to right? A question you still haven't answered? Do you see it as one long thread, or as chapters? Does it feel personal-and-small, or does it want to connect to something bigger?"

This is not a multiple choice. The user's instinct about how it should feel almost always contains the structure. A "confession" essay has a different arc than a "lesson" essay. A "question I still haven't answered" essay ends differently than a "journey from wrong to right."

Reflect back what you heard:

> "So it sounds like you want this to feel like [restate]. That suggests a structure where [describe the arc in plain language]. Does that feel right, or does it feel off in any direction?"

Only after this conversation — when you have the real reason, the personal story, the avoided sentence, and a sense of how it should feel — do you move into the steps below.

## The Process

### Step 0 — Find the avoided sentence (before anything else)

This is the step most writing frameworks skip. Do it first.

Ask the user:

> "What is the sentence in this essay you are most reluctant to write? The one that feels too exposed, too honest, too small, or too obvious to say out loud?"

That sentence is almost always the center of the essay. Everything else — the anchor story, the arc, the research — is scaffolding around it. If the writer builds the scaffolding first, they almost always build it around a safer subject than the real one.

Lamott calls it the shitty first draft — the one you write to find out what you actually think. Didion says she writes to find out what she is afraid of. Strayed says the sentence you avoid is the one the essay cannot be written without.

Do not move to Step 1 until this sentence exists, even roughly.

---

### Step 1 — Surface the real idea (metacognition)

Ask:

> "What is the thing you actually want people to *feel or understand* — not the topic, but the insight underneath it? And what personal experience made you believe this?"

Push for specificity. "I want people to understand AI is risky" is a topic. "I started taking notes while using AI because I noticed I was nodding at outputs I didn't actually understand — and that saved me from some real messes" is an insight. The difference is the lived experience underneath.

If the user is vague, ask: *When did you first notice this? Was there a specific moment — a meeting, a failure, a conversation — where this became real for you?*

Also ask: *What did you stand to lose if this insight had not arrived when it did?* Without a cost, there is no story — only a reflection.

Write down what they say verbatim. Do not polish it yet.

---

### Step 2 — Find the anchor story (fable, history, or myth)

Every essay starts with an external story that earns the personal one. The anchor story should:
- Be ancient, well-known, or from a different world than the essay's topic
- Contain the same insight the user wants to convey — but in a different domain
- Have a detail most people overlook (the part nobody asks about)

Run a research agent to find the best match. Search across:
- Indian epics and mythology (Mahabharata, Ramayana, Panchatantra, Upanishads, Jataka tales)
- Greek and Roman mythology
- Historical figures and overlooked moments
- Fables from any tradition

The framing is always: *everyone knows X about this story, but nobody asks Y.* The Y is the real lesson. Find a story where Y maps to the user's insight.

If the user already has a story in mind, validate it and find the overlooked detail.

---

### Step 3 — Build the personal arc (chronological origin first)

Map the user's personal experiences in chronological order. The essay's spine:

1. **Origin** — where did this habit, belief, or insight first appear? (Childhood, school, early career)
2. **Carried forward** — how did it persist and evolve?
3. **Tested** — when did a new context (COVID, a new job, a new tool) challenge it?
4. **Named** — when did the user finally find vocabulary or research that confirmed what they had been doing all along?
5. **New application** — how does the insight apply to the current problem?

At each stage, push for a **physical scene** — not just an observation. Where were you? What did you see, hear, or feel? Readers do not inhabit ideas. They inhabit a body in a place. One sensory detail that only you could have noticed — a specific smell, a light, a physical sensation — earns more trust than three paragraphs of reflection.

Ask the user to fill in each stage with a specific moment, not a general observation.

Key rule: **the insight should come from the user's own experience first, then be validated by research** — never the other way around. Research confirms instinct. It does not replace it.

---

### Step 4 — Research validation (optional but powerful)

Once the personal arc is clear, run a research agent to find:

- Academic papers or studies that name what the user was experiencing
- Industry research that documents the problem the user solved instinctively
- Prior art — has anyone written about this specific angle before? (If not, that is the whitespace the essay occupies)
- Vocabulary the user did not have — often the most satisfying moment in an essay is "I had been doing this all along, I just never had a name for it"

Verify all claims and statistics. Do not use a number that cannot be sourced.

Keep research out of the main essay body. It goes in the postscript.

---

### Step 5 — Structure like a storyteller

The essay arc:

```
Opening — the anchor story, the overlooked detail, the question nobody asks
Bridge — "I have been thinking about this. I think I finally understand why."
Section 1 — the origin of the insight (earliest personal memory, physical scene)
Section 2 — how the habit built and was carried forward
Section 3 — when it was tested in a new context
Section 4 — the new problem (the current thing the essay is really about)
Section 5 — the solution: the old insight applied to the new problem
Closing — return to the anchor story image, tight, no list, one final punch
```

Rules:
- The opening pays off in the closing. Always return to the image you opened with.
- Each section ends with forward momentum — the reader should want to know what happens next.
- At every section break, ask: *why should a stranger keep reading here?* If you cannot answer, cut or reorder.
- Short sentences land harder than long ones. Use them at turning points.
- The user's failures and confessions are features, not bugs. They are what makes the reader trust the writer.
- If a real conversation was part of the moment, use the actual words or the closest honest reconstruction. Paraphrase keeps readers outside. Direct speech puts them in the room.

---

### Step 6 — Edit like an editor

After drafting, three separate passes:

**Structural pass:**
- Is the chronology right? Origin before consequence. Cause before effect.
- Are there two sections making the same point? Merge them.
- Does every section move the story forward, or does any section just summarize?
- Does the closing echo the opening image?
- Does the avoided sentence from Step 0 appear in the essay — or has it been edited out? If it is gone, the essay has gotten safer. Put it back.

**Line-level pass:**
- Find every sentence that begins with "I think" or "I believe" and cut the preamble.
- Find every paragraph that ends with a general statement and replace it with a specific image or line of speech.
- Find the smoothest, most AI-sounding sentences and rough them up. The user's voice has texture.
- Cut any sentence that explains the metaphor. Trust the reader.
- Anchor each key scene in one physical detail — a smell, a light, a specific sound. Ideas are portable. Scenes are yours.

**Length pass:**
- Target: ~1500 words, ~7 min read for the main essay.
- Anything interesting but not essential to the main arc goes to postscript.
- Research, context, side-stories, and external references always go to postscript.

---

### Step 7 — Postscripts over footnotes

Two types of postscript:

**Postscript 1 — The story behind the story:** How the user came to write this. The context that doesn't fit the main arc but rewards the curious reader. The moment that sparked the question.

**Postscript 2 — The research:** All named studies, statistics, vocabulary, and prior art. Framed as: *"I started doing X by instinct. Then I went looking for proof. Here is what I found."*

Postscripts are not required reading. They are a gift for readers who want to go deeper.

## Badal's writing voice (applied to any user)

These are the principles extracted from Badal's published essays. Apply them unless the user has a different voice:

- **Find the avoided sentence first.** The essay is almost always about the thing you did not plan to say.
- **First person, present tense confessions.** Not "one might argue" — "I was wrong."
- **Short punchy sentences at turning points.** Save long sentences for building context.
- **Name real people.** Not "a colleague" or "my manager" — their actual name. Named people make scenes feel witnessed, not constructed. Ask the user: who was actually in the room?
- **Specific over general.** Not "a difficult meeting" — "a steering call with their CTO, Mahesh, with my leaders Sri, Renu, Atul on the line."
- **The quiet integrity beat.** Find the small private act no one was supposed to know about — something the writer did when no one was watching, for no external reward. "I'd default to ARM in the IaC. No one asked me to. The customer didn't know. But I knew." This is the most trustworthy sentence type in a personal essay. Ask the user: is there something you did quietly, without credit, that belongs here?
- **The "part I don't talk about enough" move.** One section that drops the professional narrative and says the thing usually edited out — a doubt carried silently, a personal cost, something the writer normally keeps private. Signals genuine honesty, not performed honesty.
- **Physical scenes, not just mental observations.** One sensory detail grounds the reader in your body, your moment.
- **Real speech where it happened.** The exact words someone said. Not a paraphrase.
- **Humor is quick and self-aware.** One line. Never a setup-punchline structure.
- **Failures are told cleanly, without wallowing.** State it, move on.
- **The anchor doesn't have to be ancient.** A childhood question that followed you for a decade works as well as a myth. What matters: it comes from a different time or world than the main subject, and contains the overlooked detail nobody asks about.
- **End with a question to the reader, not just an image.** Sometimes the most powerful closing hands the essay back to the reader and makes them the subject: "What is the question you will be glad you asked twelve years from now?"
- **Endings do not summarize.** They land on one final image, one direct instruction, or one question for the reader.
- **No AI-smooth prose.** If a paragraph reads like it could have been generated, it probably was. Rough it up.
- **Reader first at every structural decision.** Ask: why should a stranger keep reading? Not: have I said everything I wanted to say?

## Closing conventions

Two things that appear in every published essay — do not skip them:

**The dedication.** End with a note to real, named people. Not generic thanks — specific people, specific reasons. "She read it first." "You reached out after the last one. It mattered more than you know." This turns a public essay into something that also functions as a letter.

**Full disclosure on AI.** Add a closing line on how AI was used. Something like: *"This was drafted by hand but refined with AI. That is the point — optimization, not substitution. Every memory, every person named, every failure is real."* In a world where readers wonder, this is an integrity move, not a disclaimer.

## How to invoke this skill

Type `/storyteller` in Claude Code, then describe your idea in one or two sentences — even rough, even uncertain. The skill will ask you the right questions from there.

Or pass your idea directly:

```
/storyteller I want to write about how I stopped trusting my own judgment when AI arrived and had to find my way back to it.
```

The skill handles the rest.
