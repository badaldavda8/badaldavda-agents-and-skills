# How to Actually Be a Human in the Loop

---

There is a phrase that gets used a lot in AI circles: *human in the loop*. It sounds like a safeguard. It sounds like you. It sounds like the thing standing between a capable model and an unchecked output.

Most of us are not actually that.

We are present. We are nodding. We are technically in the loop the way I was technically in every Zoom meeting during COVID — camera on, dressed for work, mind somewhere else entirely. The phrase describes a position, not a practice. And there is a difference.

I want to tell you about an ancient story that clarified this for me. And about a habit I nearly lost — and what happened when I lost it.

---

Most people know one thing about Ekalavya: Dronacharya asked for his thumb.

The tribal boy. The royal teacher. The demand. The thumb cut off without hesitation. Tragedy. Injustice. End of story.

But nobody ever asks the question that should come before all of that.

*How did he get so good?*

Drona had refused to teach him. Wrong lineage. Wrong background. Door closed. The Kuru princes — Arjuna, Bhima, Duryodhana — had years of daily, structured, personal training from the greatest archery teacher alive. Ekalavya had none of that.

So how did a boy with no teacher, no peers, no training ground become so formidable that the greatest teacher of his age felt *threatened*?

He went back into the forest. And he did something the Mahabharata describes almost as a footnote, even though it is the entire point.

He built a clay statue of Dronacharya. Bowed to it as his guru. And practiced in front of it every single day.

Not a real teacher. A *representation* of the teacher. Every session, he had to reconstruct in his own mind what Drona would say, what Drona would correct, where Drona would point. He had to be the student and the teacher simultaneously. He had to stay awake to his own practice because there was nobody else in the forest to catch his mistakes.

That is why he became extraordinary. Not despite the absence of a teacher. *Because* of what that absence forced him to do.

I have been thinking about that clay statue for a while now. I think I finally understand why.

## The Notebook I Hated and Then Grew Fond of

I was never a consistent 8 GPA or 9 GPA student. Occasionally I was. I choose to remember those occasions more than the others. But curious — always. Always asking questions. Always writing them down.

What I could not stand was copying notes in school. Early morning classes in summer. No AC at home, so the school classroom felt like a reward just for showing up — cool air, ceiling fans, the smell of chalk. And I would sit there, half-dreaming, eyes open, hand moving across the page, copying whatever the teacher wrote on the board. Blindly. Still half asleep. Your hand moves. Your brain does not. I despised it. It felt like the opposite of learning — going through the motions of paying attention while actually doing nothing at all.

So I stopped doing it that way. What I did instead — and still do — was read the reference material first. Sit with it. Try to understand it. Then make my own notes. Not a copy of what someone else said. A record of what *I* had understood, what connections I had made, what questions I still had.

The notes looked messier. They were shorter. But they were mine.

That distinction — between copying and reconstructing — turns out to be everything. When you copy, your hand does the work. When you reconstruct, your brain does. Only one of those produces understanding.

---

## IIT and the Habit That Stuck

I failed the IIT entrance exam. Twice. I have made peace with the second failure. The first one I am still not sure about.

I am not saying the note-taking habit would have gotten me in. But during those years of prep, that habit was the only thing that made the material feel like it was actually landing. I would read a concept, sit with it, then write what I understood — not what the textbook said, but what *I* now believed to be true and why. I would pick up a term from a lecture, chase where it connected to something I already knew, scribble the connection before it disappeared.

It carried into my working years. Not every meeting — I am not taking notes during a quick sync about deployment timings. But the ones that demand the most: requirements gathering, working through a solution out loud, a lecture with no slides to fall back on. Those meetings, I need to talk to myself on paper. Pen and a spiral notebook, both on my desk — or the laptop if I cannot. My hand forces my brain to stay just slightly ahead of it. And when I stop being able to write anything — when nothing is connecting — that is the signal. I have lost the thread. Ask a question.

Not transcription. Never transcription. Reconstruction.

I did not think of this as a system. It was just what I did. Like the school boy who stopped copying from the board — not out of rebellion, but because copying felt like nothing, and I wanted to feel something land.

---

## COVID, Zoom, and the Problem of Being Present Without Being There

When work-from-home started, I joined every meeting. Camera off, muted, mind somewhere else entirely. Physically present. Mentally gone.

I noticed this and fixed it the painful way: I started getting dressed for work before joining calls. Same clothes I would wear to the office. If I am on camera, I have to look like I am there. Looking like I am there forces me to act like I am there.

It helped. Somewhat.

Then I noticed I was on camera, dressed, nodding — and still phasing out. Staring at my own face in the corner tile. Half-listening. You can see people on a Zoom call. You can hear them. But you are not quite *with* them the way physical presence forces you to be. The friction is gone. And without friction, attention drifts.

The dressing-up trick added friction at the start. The pen sustained it through the call.

The moment I started taking notes in meetings again — not transcribing, reconstructing — I stopped phasing out. Same habit. New context.

Then one night scrolling Instagram at midnight, I came across a reel about *active recall*. The science behind why retrieval and reconstruction makes things stick — not re-reading, not highlighting, not copying. The act of rebuilding from your own understanding is what creates the understanding.

I thought: I have been doing this my whole life. I just never had a name for it.

---

## Then AI Arrived, and I Got Lazy

Here is the thing about AI that nobody warns you about upfront: it is *very* agreeable.

You tell it something. It agrees. You tell it a slightly wrong version of something. It agrees with more confidence. You bury a bad assumption inside a prompt. It builds a beautiful, well-structured answer on top of that assumption — and now that assumption is in the context. From that point forward, every time you continue the conversation, AI carries it forward. Faithfully. Relentlessly. It treats what you said as ground truth because you said it and it never pushed back.

I walked into some genuine messes this way. I called it "letting AI do its thing." What it actually was: I set up a context with assumptions I never questioned, stepped back, and let it run. Hours later I had pages of output — structured, confident, well-formatted — that were built entirely on what AI had silently decided I meant. Not what I had said. What it had inferred. And because I had not been tracking the reasoning, I could not even tell you at which point the thread had gone wrong. It is not like a colleague who might say in passing: *"wait, are we still sure about X?"* AI just carries the context forward. One bad assumption early in a conversation spreads the way water gets into walls — invisible until the damage is deep.

You are not talking to a skeptic. You are talking to the world's most capable yes-person.

And I was reading its output the same way I used to copy notes from the board. My eyes moving. My brain off. Nodding at the answer without following the reasoning. At some point I looked up and thought: *I have no idea what this thing just decided on my behalf.*

Same failure. Different decade. Different tool.

Here is the honest version I do not say often enough: I was telling myself I was being efficient. Delegating the grunt work. Working smarter. But what I was actually doing was outsourcing my thinking and calling it a system.

---

## The Clay Statue, Revisited

So I did what I had always done. Almost by instinct, I opened a notebook next to my laptop when working with AI.

I wrote down what I was asking it to do. The assumptions I was bringing in — because those are the ones that anchor everything that follows. I noted when something surprised me, when a response felt too smooth, when AI agreed too fast. I tried — in my own words, in my own handwriting — to reconstruct what AI just said.

When I stop being able to do that, I know I have drifted. I am Zoom-call-me again. Camera on. Not there.

The reconstruction is not for the notes. The reconstruction *is* the understanding.

And if you want the shortcut: ask AI to build the statue for you.

After a complex response, try:

> *"Summarise what you just did as a mind map."*

> *"Show me your reasoning as a flowchart."*

> *"What assumptions did you make, and what conclusions did they lead to?"*

A diagram of AI's reasoning is the clay statue. It makes the invisible thinking visible. You will spot in thirty seconds what you would miss in three readings of the prose — a missing branch, a conclusion floating free of any evidence, an assumption that has quietly become a load-bearing wall.

Same principle as the notebook. Just outsourced. Instead of you building the representation, you ask AI to build it. Either way, the thinking becomes something you can stand in front of and push back on.

---

## The Thumb Was Never the Point

Every retelling of Ekalavya starts at the thumb. The cruelty. The sacrifice. That is the part we remember.

But the thumb is the end of the story. What stays with me is the beginning — a boy alone in a forest, standing before a clay statue he built with his own hands, practicing in the absence of everything: no teacher, no peers, no validation, no feedback loop except the one he constructed for himself.

He could have watched the princes practice from the treeline. Passively. Safely. He chose not to.

Most of us use AI from the treeline. We read the output. We nod. We move on. We copy from the board.

Presence is not passive. It is a practice.

Pick up a pen. Build the statue. Don't let AI construct on sand while you stand there nodding at it.

## Postscript 1 — Where I First Heard "Human in the Loop"

I was reading the AWS blog on the [AI-Driven Development Lifecycle](https://aws.amazon.com/blogs/devops/ai-driven-development-life-cycle/) — AI-DLC — a framework built around a reassuring idea: humans stay in control. AI creates a plan, asks clarifying questions, implements only after human validation. One of its two core dimensions is literally called *"AI Powered Execution with Human Oversight."* Only humans, the framework says, possess the contextual understanding needed to make informed choices.

I read that and nodded. Yes. Human in the loop. That is me. I am the loop.

Then I thought about how I was actually using AI day to day.

I was in the loop the way I was on those early Zoom calls. Present. Nodding. Not there. A rubber stamp with a heartbeat.

The phrase sounds like a safeguard. It only works if the human is actually engaged — tracking the reasoning, catching the bad assumptions before they become load-bearing walls. That question — *how do I actually be a good human in the loop, not just a present one* — is what sent me back to the notebook.

---

## Postscript 2 — I Went Looking for Proof

When I started keeping the notebook open next to AI, I was not sure if I was just being sentimental. Old habit. Was this actually useful, or was I the person who insists on a paper map because GPS feels lazy?

So I went looking.

What I found was a body of research that had named everything I had been experiencing — just in vocabulary I did not have yet.

The AI agreeing with my wrong assumptions has a name: *context anchoring*. Research has found that spreading wrong information across a multi-turn conversation caused an average 39% performance drop in outputs (arxiv.org/pdf/2505.06120). The model anchors to what was said early and cannot fully recover even when corrected later.

The deeper yes-man problem has a name too: *AI sycophancy*. Large language models are structurally designed to produce contextually agreeable responses. They are not neutral. They are optimised to make you feel heard. That is the feature that becomes the bug.

And then there is the part that should be on a warning label. A University of Pennsylvania study (SSRN, 2024) found that students using ChatGPT solved 48% more practice problems — but scored 17% *lower* on tests of actual conceptual understanding. Doing more. Understanding less. Researcher Ethan Mollick calls this the *jagged frontier problem*: users cannot locate where AI competence ends, so they defer to it everywhere — including where it is confidently wrong.

I was not being sentimental about notebooks. I was accidentally applying active recall to a problem that researchers had documented but not yet prescribed a practical solution for.

The instinct was right. It just needed a name.

## Postscript 3 — Prompts That Have Actually Worked for Me

These are the prompts I keep coming back to. Not frameworks. Not systems. Just things I noticed worked, usually after noticing something else had stopped working.

---

**When I want to actually understand something, not just know it**

I ask AI to explain it as a story in a made-up world.

Not "explain this concept simply." That gets you a textbook summary. This gets you something your brain can hold:

> *"Explain how distributed consensus works as if it's a story about a group of strangers trying to agree on where to eat dinner, in a world where none of them can hear each other clearly."*

The made-up world forces the model to find the actual shape of the idea rather than reciting the definition. And it forces me to follow a narrative rather than scan a list. If the story breaks down — if the metaphor stops holding — that is where my understanding has a gap. That is where I ask another question.

Or if I am in for a ride:

> *"Explain how Vector Embeddings works as if it's a story with \<xyz\> genre. Don't tell me about the concept first. Map the concept later."*

Replace `<xyz>` with mystery, crime, horror, etc.

---

**When I want AI to challenge me instead of agree with me**

I used to start sessions with: *"Keep asking me clarifying questions until I tell you to stop."*

It works. It breaks the yes-person dynamic early. When AI asks you a question, you have to think. When AI gives you an answer, you often do not.

I later found out Andrej Karpathy formalised this as a skill called *grill-me* — relentless clarifying questions until every assumption is surfaced. I had built a worse version of the same thing by trial and error. His is cleaner. The instinct was the same.

---

**When I am stuck in a troubleshooting loop**

This is the one I use most. When something is broken and AI keeps suggesting the same fix in different words, I give it a structured search order:

> *"Before suggesting another fix: check the actual code first, then the official documentation, then Reddit for known issues with this version, then AWS re:Post if this is an AWS service problem. Tell me what you find at each step before recommending anything."*

The key word is *before*. Without it, AI skips straight to a suggestion. With it, it has to show its work — and half the time, the answer is in the documentation it would have skipped.

---

**When I need to understand a system I did not build**

I do not ask for a diagram. I ask for a story.

> *"Walk me through this architecture flow by flow, like you are narrating what happens to a single request from the moment it arrives. Tell me every decision point, every handoff, every place something could go wrong."*

Diagrams show structure. Narration shows behaviour. The system makes sense to me when I can follow a request through it — when I can picture what is happening at each step and why. Flow by flow. One request at a time.

This is also how I catch things. A diagram does not tell you what happens when the queue backs up. A narrated story does, because you have to say what happens next.

---

**When I need to understand a customer's business before walking into a room**

I do not ask AI to summarise the company. Summaries give you facts. Facts do not help you ask good questions.

I ask for the founding story as a case study:

> *"Tell me the real story of how this company came to exist. Who founded it, what problem did they personally run into, why did that problem matter enough to leave whatever they were doing, and what did the first version of their solution look like? Then tell me how it became what it is today — the turns, the pivots, the moment it stopped being a bet and started being a business. If the founding story is not publicly documented, look at their website, their product, their services — and construct the most plausible version of the story that fits what they built. Make it digestible. Tell it like a story, not a profile."*

If there is a real founding story — a named founder, a documented origin, a known pivot — I ask AI to ground it there. If there is not, the constructed version is still useful: AI has to reason from what the product actually solves back to the person who must have needed it first. That reasoning is the understanding. Not a Wikipedia summary. The story of the person who saw the problem first and could not stop thinking about it — real or reconstructed.

This does something a company overview cannot: it tells you what the business is actually *for*. When you know why something was built, you understand its architecture — technical and organisational — in a way that no product page explains. You walk into the room knowing what they are proud of, what they are still trying to prove, and what keeps the founders awake.

It also makes the conversation different. Instead of asking about their tech stack, you can ask about the problem that started everything — and watch what happens to the room when you do. It makes you part of their story and their team, and motivates you to do the best for the customer.

---

None of these are revolutionary. They are all the same move in different clothes: making the invisible visible, forcing the reasoning out into the open, staying one step ahead of the output instead of waiting for it to arrive.

Ekalavya did not watch Drona teach. He reconstructed the teaching. These prompts are how I do the same thing.
